# How to Prepare for a Business Advisory Consultation: A Practical Checklist

The businesses that get the most out of an advisory consultation aren't necessarily the biggest or most established — they're the ones that show up prepared. A well-prepared first session lets a consultant understand your situation quickly and spend the time on solutions rather than discovery. Here's how to make the most of yours.

## Before the Session

**1. Write down the actual problem, not just the symptom.**
"Sales are down" is a symptom. "We've lost three of our five largest clients in the last two quarters and don't know why" is a problem a consultant can work with. Spend fifteen minutes writing out what's really happening in specific, concrete terms.

**2. Gather your basic numbers.**
You don't need a polished financial model — but having rough figures on hand (revenue trend, cost structure, headcount, cash position) turns a vague conversation into a grounded one. If you don't have these organized yet, say so; a good advisor will help you get there.

**3. List what you've already tried.**
Advisors can move faster when they know what hasn't worked, and why. This avoids re-suggesting approaches you've already ruled out.

**4. Clarify your own goal for the engagement.**
Are you looking for a one-time strategic recommendation, ongoing hands-on support, or help executing a plan you already have? Naming this upfront shapes the whole engagement.

**5. Identify who else needs to be in the room.**
If a decision requires buy-in from a co-founder, board, or department head, involve them from the first conversation where possible — advisory recommendations land better when the people who have to act on them were part of shaping them.

## Questions Worth Asking Your Advisor

- What similar situations have you worked on before, and what happened?
- What would you need from us to move quickly?
- What's a realistic timeline for seeing results, and how will we know it's working?
- What happens if the first approach doesn't work — is there a fallback plan?

## Red Flags to Watch For (On Either Side)

A consultation that promises guaranteed outcomes without understanding your specifics first is worth a second look — good advisory work is grounded in your actual numbers and constraints, not a generic template. Equally, be ready to be transparent about your own numbers and past decisions; advisors can only be as useful as the information they're working with.

## After the Session

Ask for a short written summary of what was discussed and agreed, even for an informal first consultation. It keeps both sides accountable and gives you something concrete to act on rather than a memory of the conversation.
